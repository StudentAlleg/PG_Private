# Project Green — C-Corp Naming Candidates

**Prepared by:** Richard (Board Chair, Project Green)
**Date:** March 17, 2026
**Status:** RECOMMENDATION — Awaiting Chairman decision
**Purpose:** Entity name selection for Delaware C-Corp formation

---

## Strategic Naming Criteria

The name must serve the company from incorporation through IPO at the 7-year horizon. It will appear on contracts with conservative accounting firms, on pitch decks for institutional investors, and eventually on a stock ticker. The name carries weight at every stage — it cannot be cute, clever, or disposable.

**Requirements distilled:**

| Criterion | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|
| Conveys intelligence and clarity | Accounting firms buy trust, not novelty |
| Scales beyond accounting | Vision spans all professional services, then healthcare and law |
| Works as company name AND product name | "Powered by [X]" must sound right on a partner's website |
| Domain available (.com or close variant) | Non-negotiable for enterprise credibility |
| Not limited by a technology metaphor | "Digital twin" is a feature, not a brand — the concept may evolve |
| Institutional gravitas | Must pass the "would a managing partner sign this contract?" test |

## Naming Candidates

### 1. Clearfold

**Rationale:** "Clear" evokes transparency and clarity — the core promise of the product. "Fold" suggests layers, structure, and the act of integrating complexity into something manageable. A professional service firm's data is folded into an intelligent layer. No existing software companies found under this name. Domain landscape is open (clearfold.com, clearfold.ai likely available).

**Tone:** Modern, clean, trustworthy. Reads like a company that simplifies complexity.

---

### 2. Firmgraph

**Rationale:** "Firm" directly addresses the customer (professional service firms) while "Graph" evokes knowledge graphs, data relationships, and intelligence mapping. The product literally graphs a firm's operations into a queryable intelligence layer. No existing companies found under this name.

**Tone:** Technical but accessible. Immediately communicates what it does and who it serves.

---

### 3. Twinlayer

**Rationale:** Directly references the digital twin concept ("twin") and the product's positioning as an "operating intelligence layer." Two of the most important strategic words combined. Clean, original. No existing companies found under this name.

**Tone:** Forward-looking, technical, specific. Strong for early-stage differentiation but may feel narrow at IPO scale.

---

### 4. Inquiris

**Rationale:** Latin root — "inquirere" (to seek, to examine). Evokes inquiry, investigation, and intelligence. The product is fundamentally an inquiry engine — ask your firm a question in plain English, get an intelligent answer. Elegant, distinctive, and linguistically grounded. No existing companies found under this name.

**Tone:** Sophisticated, institutional, timeless. Would look right on a stock ticker (INQR).

---

### 5. Corthen

**Rationale:** Derived from "cortex" (the brain's outer layer — where higher intelligence lives) combined with a suffix that gives it a proper noun feel. The product IS the cortex layer on top of existing firm software. No existing companies found under this name.

**Tone:** Futuristic, strong, distinctive. Sounds like a company that builds intelligence infrastructure.

---

### 6. Clarityx

**Rationale:** "Clarity" is the single most important thing this product delivers — clear answers from chaotic firm data. The "x" suffix adds a modern tech edge without being whimsical. Note: ClarityX exists in India (IT services) and as a defunct gene-testing company in Miami. The tech services company is small and geographically distant, but trademark collision risk is non-zero. Would need legal clearance.

**Tone:** Direct, modern, immediately communicates the value proposition.

---

### 7. Operis

**Rationale:** Latin for "of the work" (genitive of "opus"). The product is literally the intelligence of the work — it ingests all operational data and makes it queryable. Clean, short, memorable. "Operis Technologies" or just "Operis" both work. Light trademark landscape — no major software competitors found.

**Tone:** Classical, institutional, understated. The kind of name a firm partner trusts.

---

### 8. Meridian Intelligence

**Rationale:** A meridian is a line of reference — a fixed point from which everything is measured. The product becomes the firm's meridian, the reference point for all decision-making. "Intelligence" is explicit about what the product delivers. Note: "Meridian" is used across industries (healthcare, fintech) but "Meridian Intelligence" as a combined entity appears open.

**Tone:** Authoritative, geographic/scientific metaphor, institutional weight.

---

### 9. Palantir-adjacent consideration: Tessera

**Rationale:** A tessera is a single tile in a mosaic — the product takes thousands of data fragments (tesserae) from firm software and composes them into a complete operational picture. Distinctive, visual, memorable. Literary and historical resonance without being obscure.

**Tone:** Elegant, intellectual, visual. Strong brand storytelling potential.

---

### 10. Veridian Systems

**Rationale:** From "veritas" (truth) + suffix suggesting technology. The product delivers truth — accurate, queryable intelligence from firm data. "Systems" grounds it as an enterprise technology company. Note: "Veridian" has been used in various contexts (Veridian Credit Union, Veridian Dynamics from the TV show "Better Off Ted") but "Veridian Systems" as a technology entity appears open.

**Tone:** Clean, trustworthy, enterprise-grade.

---

### 11. Phronis

**Rationale:** Derived from "phronesis" — Aristotle's concept of practical wisdom, the ability to discern the right course of action in particular circumstances. Not abstract intelligence, but *practical* intelligence applied to real decisions. This is exactly what the product delivers. No existing companies found.

**Tone:** Deeply intellectual, distinctive, conversation-starting. May require explanation, which can be a feature (brand storytelling) or a friction point (enterprise sales).

---

### 12. Canopy Intelligence

**Rationale:** A canopy is the uppermost layer — the product sits above all existing firm software as the intelligence canopy. Also evokes protection and oversight. "Canopy" alone is taken (tax software company) but "Canopy Intelligence" appears open. Accounting-adjacent people will know Canopy Tax, which creates both familiarity and potential confusion.

**Tone:** Natural, protective, layered. Immediately understood.

---

### 13. Stratum

**Rationale:** A stratum is a layer — the product is the intelligence stratum on top of existing systems. Geological precision. Clean, one-word, memorable. Light competitive landscape for this exact name in enterprise software.

**Tone:** Scientific, authoritative, structural. Strong one-word brand.

---

### 14. Lumis

**Rationale:** From "lumen" (light). The product illuminates what's buried in firm data. Short, clean, phonetically strong. Avoids the overcrowded "Lumin-" space (Luminary, Luminance, etc.) by using a tighter form.

**Tone:** Clean, bright, modern. Works globally.

## Top 3 Recommendations

### Recommendation #1: Clearfold

**Why this is the strongest candidate:**

- Immediately communicates the value proposition: clarity from complexity
- "Fold" is unusual enough to be distinctive but intuitive enough to need no explanation
- Works at every scale: "Clearfold" on a seed deck, "Clearfold" on an S-1 filing, "Clearfold" on a conference badge
- Passes the managing partner test: a conservative firm partner would sign a contract with Clearfold without flinching
- Works as both company and product: "The Clearfold platform" / "Powered by Clearfold"
- Clean trademark landscape — no competing entities found
- Domain likely available in .com or .ai variants
- Does not lock the company into accounting, AI, or any single technology trend

**Ticker potential:** CFLD

---

### Recommendation #2: Inquiris

**Why this is the second choice:**

- Linguistically grounded, institutionally elegant — the kind of name that ages well
- Directly captures the product's core interaction model: inquiry-based intelligence
- Distinctive enough to own search results from day one
- Classical roots signal seriousness to conservative buyers
- Works internationally (Latin roots are universally parsed)
- Clean competitive landscape

**Risk:** Pronunciation ambiguity (in-KWIR-is vs. in-kwi-RIS). Must be established early and consistently. This is manageable but real.

**Ticker potential:** INQR

---

### Recommendation #3: Firmgraph

**Why this is the third choice:**

- Maximum clarity about what the product does and who it serves
- "Graph" is a power word in the data/AI space (knowledge graph, social graph) — positions the product in the right mental category for technical buyers
- Zero ambiguity in enterprise sales conversations
- Clean competitive landscape

**Risk:** "Firm" may feel limiting once the product expands beyond professional service firms into healthcare systems and other verticals. This name is the strongest for the beachhead market but the weakest for the 10x-scale question. A name change or parent-brand strategy may be needed at expansion — and name changes at scale are expensive.

**Ticker potential:** FMGR

## Names to Avoid (Common Pitfalls)

### 1. Anything with "AI" in the name
Examples: *FirmAI, GreenAI, TwinAI*

AI is a feature, not a brand. Every company will claim AI within 24 months. Names with "AI" age poorly — they sound like 2024 when it's 2030. They also become invisible in a saturated market. Palantir, Snowflake, Databricks — none of them have "AI" or "data" in the name. Build a brand, not a feature label.

### 2. Anything with "Green" in the name
Examples: *GreenTwin, Greenfield Systems, Green Intelligence*

"Green" is the project codename and should stay internal. Using it externally ties the brand to an arbitrary color with heavy ESG/sustainability connotations that have nothing to do with the product. It will confuse the market.

### 3. FPTech
The domain fptech.com is owned by fP Technologies, Inc., an Ohio corporation selling database management tools since the 1990s. Even if the domain were available, "FPTech" is generic, forgettable, and does not communicate the product's value. It sounds like a consulting firm, not a platform company valued at $100M+.

### 4. Anything ending in "-ify" or "-ly"
Examples: *Firmify, Clarify.ly, Twinly*

These suffixes signal consumer SaaS, not enterprise infrastructure. Managing partners at accounting firms will not take a product called "Firmify" seriously. These names are designed for PLG motions and SMB buyers — the opposite of the target market.

### 5. Compound words that sound like other products
Examples: *DataTwin, SmartFirm, FirmOS*

Trademark collisions, SEO nightmares, and zero distinctiveness. "FirmOS" sounds like it was generated by a name generator in 2023. These names create confusion and legal exposure.

### 6. Names that require explanation of a foreign language
Examples: *Phronesis Technologies, Episteme Systems*

While classical roots are powerful when abbreviated or adapted (see: Inquiris, Operis), full foreign words create friction in sales conversations. If you have to explain the name before you can explain the product, you've added a step to every deal.

### 7. Names already owned by well-funded competitors in adjacent spaces
Avoid: *Verdant* (Verdant AI exists, $0 funding but active), *Parallax* (multiple companies, $12M+ raised), *Axiom* (just raised $200M), *Luminary* (multiple active companies in adjacent markets including estate planning for CPA firms — direct collision), *Curia* (acquired by Aledade in healthcare AI), *Canopy* alone (Canopy Tax is a known brand in accounting tech)
