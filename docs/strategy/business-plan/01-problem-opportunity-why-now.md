# Section 1: Problem, Opportunity & Why Now

*Business plan section 1 — Project Green*

---

## 1. The Problem: A Structural Crisis, Not a Cyclical One

The US accounting industry is not in a temporary downturn. It is in a permanent workforce and operational transformation. Firms cannot hire their way out of it.

### The Talent Crisis

- **Retirement wave:** Nearly three-quarters of CPAs are at or approaching retirement age. The pipeline is not recovering: CPA exam candidates have declined sharply, and the 150-hour education requirement discourages new entrants.
- **Knowledge drain:** When senior staff leave, decades of institutional knowledge—how the firm handles edge cases, client history, unwritten procedures—leave with them. Junior staff onboarding takes months because so much is undocumented.
- **Partner bottleneck:** Partners spend a large share of their day answering questions their team should be able to answer: policy lookups, procedural guidance, “how did we handle this last time?” The most expensive resource in the firm is consumed by repetitive, low-leverage work.

### The Operational Impact

- **Inconsistent answers** erode client trust and create liability when different staff give different guidance.
- **Slow responsiveness:** Clients ask “Why did my profit drop?” or “What’s my AR balance?” and wait days for an answer that could be instant if the firm’s data were queryable in one place.
- **No single view:** Firms run on QuickBooks, payroll systems, tax software, document stores, and spreadsheets. No product today connects all of them into a unified intelligence layer. Questions that span systems go unanswered or require manual assembly.

### Why Current Substitutes Fail

| Substitute | Why it fails |
|------------|--------------|
| Ask a senior | Bottleneck, interruption, inconsistent answers; doesn’t scale. |
| Search QBO / SharePoint | Slow, limited to one system, often fruitless. |
| Manual dashboards | Stale by the time they’re built; don’t answer ad hoc questions. |
| Paste data into ChatGPT | No live connection to firm data; confidentiality risk; not firm-specific. |
| Hire more people | The talent does not exist in the volume needed. |

The industry has the budget—$145B+ in the US—but not the supply of human expertise to execute. AI is not optional; it is the only scalable response to the workforce gap.

## 2. The Opportunity: An Underserved Segment and an Unfilled Gap

### Market Size and Structure

- **80,000+ accounting firms** in the US; the vast majority are small to mid-sized (roughly 78,000 firms in the 2–50 person range).
- **AI accounting automation** is a multi-billion-dollar market growing at roughly 40% CAGR; adoption in accounting jumped from single digits to over 40% in a single year. Firms are actively looking for solutions.
- **Serviceable segment:** 10–50 person firms doing tax and bookkeeping, on cloud GL (e.g. QuickBooks Online), in reachable metros, with managing partners who feel capacity and knowledge pain. This is a multi-billion-dollar serviceable market, with early-year targets in the tens of firms and $100K+ ACV potential per firm.

### The Gap No One Fills

- **Multi-system intelligence:** No product connects a firm’s GL, payroll, tax software, and documents into one queryable layer. Point solutions automate one workflow (AP, close, bookkeeping); ERPs stay inside one ecosystem. When a partner asks “How is Client X performing across all our services?”, no tool can answer today.
- **Institutional knowledge for small firms:** When a senior leaves a 15-person firm, their knowledge walks out. Enterprise knowledge tools are too heavy; generic AI doesn’t know the firm’s procedures or data. No productized solution captures and serves that knowledge for firms this size.
- **The 78,000 small firms:** The Big Four and large vendors build for the top of the market. Startups serve small firms with narrow point solutions. The segment that needs firm-wide intelligence—same core needs as larger firms, but smaller budget and no internal IT—is underserved.

The opportunity is to be the **operating intelligence layer** for these firms: connect what they already use, ingest their data and documents, and deliver a firm-specific AI that answers questions accurately, with citations, without replacing their existing systems.

## 3. Why Now: Five Converging Forces

The problem and the opportunity are not new in the abstract. What is new is that **now** the technology, market, and structure of the industry align to make a solution viable and urgent.

### 1. Agentic AI Is Production-Ready

Multi-step, autonomous AI agents that plan, decide, act, and verify across workflows are no longer research projects. 2025–2026 is when this capability became viable for production. The leap from “chatbot” to “agent that uses your data and tools” is what makes a firm-wide assistant possible. The technology is ready.

### 2. Private Equity Is Reshaping the Industry

Private equity is rolling up accounting firms at scale. A significant share of the largest US firms are or will be PE-backed. These owners need operational clarity, standardized processes, margin optimization, and exit-ready metrics. When rates normalize and exits ramp up, firms that can demonstrate operational intelligence will command premium valuations. The demand for a “process twin” and clear operational data is accelerating.

### 3. The CPA Retirement Wave Is Biting Now

The retirement wave is no longer a future scenario; it is current. Firms cannot replace departing expertise through hiring alone. The urgency to automate, preserve knowledge, and reduce dependence on a few key people is immediate. Solutions that capture institutional knowledge and scale answers are directly aligned with this timing.

### 4. A Fragmented Market of 80,000+ Firms

Unlike enterprise software with a handful of dominant buyers, accounting is a large, fragmented market with tens of thousands of firms that share the same core needs: better use of data, faster answers, preserved knowledge. A productized solution that works for one 15-person firm can scale across many without customizing for each. The structure of the market supports a platform play.

### 5. Big Four Invest in AI but Not in This Segment

The Big Four have committed billions to AI and are building digital twins and proprietary tools—for their own practices and for the largest clients. They are not productizing for the 78,000 smaller firms. That creates awareness and demand (“we need something like that”) without supply. The gap between what the Big Four advertise and what small and mid-sized firms can access is the opening.

---

## 4. The Timing Window

The window is open but finite.

- **2025–2026:** Adoption inflection. Firms are evaluating AI; early movers can establish data moats and switching costs.
- **2027–2028:** Market consolidation. Incumbents (e.g. Intuit, Microsoft) will ship more capable AI; winners will be those already in production with paying customers.
- **2029+:** Mature market. Late entrants will face entrenched players with deep integration and accumulated data.

The imperative: **a working product and paying customers by the end of 2026, and scaling into 2027.** Project Green’s first alpha customer (February 2026) validates that the problem is real and that a firm will commit to using the product; the next step is delivery, then conversion and replication.

---

## Summary

- **Problem:** Structural CPA workforce and knowledge crisis; partners bottlenecked; no single intelligence layer across firm systems; current substitutes don’t scale.
- **Opportunity:** 80,000+ firms, a multi-billion-dollar serviceable market, and a clear gap: firm-wide, multi-system intelligence and institutional knowledge capture for small and mid-sized firms.
- **Why now:** Agentic AI is viable; PE roll-ups and exit dynamics create demand for operational intelligence; retirement-driven urgency is current; market fragmentation supports productized scale; Big Four create demand they don’t serve for this segment.
- **Timing:** Act in 2025–2026 to capture the adoption window; delay cedes the window to incumbents and faster-moving competitors.
