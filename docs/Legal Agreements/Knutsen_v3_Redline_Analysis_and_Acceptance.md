## Purpose

This document records my analysis of the eight redlines Kris proposed in v3 of the Mutual NDA & IP Assignment Agreement. I relied on this analysis to reach my decision, which is recorded at the end of this document. I am sharing this with Kris for full transparency.

---

## Redline-by-Redline Analysis

### Redline 1 — §1.1: Confidential Information Definition

**What changed:** The definition of Confidential Information now scopes Discord and direct message coverage to communications made "in connection with Project Green," rather than a blanket reference to "Discord channels."

**My analysis:** This is a tighter formulation that still captures every legitimate Project Green communication — Discord, DMs, meetings, written exchanges — while correctly excluding unrelated personal activity. The original language was slightly overbroad. This version protects what matters without overreaching.

**Verdict:** Accept.

---

### Redline 2 — §2.2: Permitted Disclosures (New Clause b)

**What changed:** Added a permitted disclosure path for employees, contractors, and professional advisors who have a legitimate need to know, provided they are bound by written confidentiality obligations no less protective than this Agreement.

**My analysis:** The original only permitted disclosure to legal counsel and government bodies. In practice, both Kris and I will need to discuss Project Green with developers, accountants, co-founders, and other advisors. Without this clause, any such conversation would be a technical breach. The written-confidentiality requirement protects both of us. I need this clause as much as Kris does.

**Verdict:** Accept. This fixes a gap in v2.

---

### Redline 3 — §2.6: Confidentiality Term (Indefinite → 5 Years)

**What changed:** General Confidential Information obligations now run for five years from the later of (a) date of disclosure or (b) termination of the Agreement, instead of indefinitely. Trade secret obligations remain perpetual.

**My analysis:** Indefinite confidentiality obligations on general business information are legally aggressive and have been challenged as unenforceable. Five years is the industry standard. Anything that qualifies as a trade secret under §1.4 — which includes the most sensitive material — remains perpetually protected. The five-year sunset only applies to general business information that naturally loses commercial sensitivity over time. This is more enforceable, not less protective.

**Verdict:** Accept.

---

### Redline 4 — §3.1: Pre-Existing IP License (Removed "Perpetual, Irrevocable")

**What changed:** The license grant on Kris's Pre-Existing IP (if incorporated into Company work product) is no longer perpetual and irrevocable. It survives termination for already-delivered and accepted work product, protects end-user rights in already-distributed products, and is terminable only upon material breach that remains uncured for 30 days after written notice.

**My analysis:** This is the most significant redline. Under v2, even if the Company materially breached — failed to pay, misappropriated Kris's confidential information, or dissolved — Kris would have no ability to withdraw the license on his own pre-existing IP. The revised version still gives the Company a durable license for everything already delivered and protects downstream customers. Kris only gains a termination right if the Company does something seriously wrong and refuses to cure it for 30 days. That is a scenario where he should have leverage over his own IP.

**Verdict:** Accept. The 30-day cure period and end-user carve-out protect the Company's legitimate interests. The termination right protects Kris's legitimate interests. This is balanced.

---

### Redline 5 — §3.1: Assignment Presumption (Scoped to Exhibit C)

**What changed:** Replaced "any IP relevant to the Company's business that is created by Knutsen and is not listed in Exhibit A is presumed to be newly created under this engagement" with language tying the assignment presumption specifically to deliverables created at the Company's written direction under Exhibit C. IP developed by Kris independently, outside Exhibit C scope, is not presumed to belong to the Company.

**My analysis:** The v2 "relevant to the Company's business" standard was too broad. An AI platform for accounting firms touches technology, finance, compliance, AI/ML, and security — that could sweep in almost anything Kris builds in adjacent fields. The Company's legitimate interest is owning what it directs and pays for under Exhibit C. If Kris builds an unrelated security product on his own time, it should not be claimed by the Company simply because security is "relevant" to an accounting platform. The revised language protects the Company's actual IP while respecting Kris's independence.

**Verdict:** Accept. This scopes assignment to its intended purpose.

---

### Redline 6 — §3.2: IP Assignment (Removed "Conceived"; Added Carve-Outs)

**What changed:** Three substantive changes:
1. Removed "conceived" — assignment now requires written direction, performance under Exhibit C, and tangible delivery.
2. Added a personal-time carve-out: IP Kris develops (i) entirely on his own time, (ii) without use of Company CI/equipment/resources, and (iii) without direct use of Company work product, belongs to Kris.
3. Kris bears the burden of proving all three criteria for the carve-out.

**My analysis:** "Conceived" was the most aggressive word in v2 — it could capture an idea Kris has while reading a Project Green document, even if he never writes it down, never delivers it, and never develops it for the Company. That is not what IP assignment should cover. The Company should own what Kris builds and delivers at the Company's direction — not what he thinks about.

The personal-time carve-out is necessary because neither Delaware (governing law) nor Colorado (Kris's state) provides statutory protection for contractor IP developed on personal time. Without this explicit carve-out, the broad Services definition combined with the assignment clause could sweep in Kris's independent work. The three-part test is commercially standard, and Kris bearing the burden of proof is fair to the Company.

**Verdict:** Accept. All three changes bring the assignment clause to where it should have been.

---

### Redline 7 — §3.4: Attorney-in-Fact (Scoped to Exhibit C)

**What changed:** The power of attorney is now limited to executing IP assignment and registration documents directly related to New IP created and delivered under Exhibit C. Explicit exclusions for Pre-Existing IP (Exhibit A), IP developed on Kris's own time under §3.2, and any IP developed outside the scope of Exhibit C.

**My analysis:** The original had no scope limitation. Theoretically, I could execute any document on Kris's behalf — including documents purporting to assign his Exhibit A pre-existing IP or independently-developed IP. That was never the intent. The power of attorney exists for one legitimate purpose: completing IP registrations for contracted deliverables Kris was already paid to create. It should be limited to that purpose.

**Verdict:** Accept. This is the correct scope.

---

### Redline 8 — §3.8: Compensation for Additional IP (New Section)

**What changed:** New section establishing that if the Company requests Kris to create IP beyond Exhibit C deliverables, it requires separate written compensation negotiation before Kris undertakes the work. The 0.25% revenue share from the Terms Memo does not constitute compensation for IP contributions beyond Exhibit C.

**My analysis:** The revenue share compensates Kris's advisory role as Board Member. It was never intended to serve as a blank check for open-ended IP assignment. If I ask Kris to build something substantial beyond the six hours in Exhibit C, that should be a separately negotiated and compensated engagement. This clause makes that expectation explicit and prevents retroactive arguments in either direction. It protects both of us.

**Verdict:** Accept.

---

## Overall Assessment

Kris's eight redlines are well-drafted, commercially reasonable, and made in good faith. They do not weaken the Company's IP protections over the work the Company is paying for. They scope IP assignment to its intended coverage — Exhibit C deliverables — and bring several provisions from "legally aggressive" to "industry standard."

The v2 language was overreaching in places I did not fully appreciate when I drafted it. Kris identified those issues clearly, explained why they matter, and proposed balanced solutions that protect both parties.

Every redline either fixes a gap (§2.2 permitted disclosures), improves enforceability (§2.6 five-year term), or scopes a provision to its legitimate purpose (§3.1, §3.2, §3.4, §3.8). None of them create new risks for the Company.

**I accept all eight redlines as proposed in v3, with no amendments.**
